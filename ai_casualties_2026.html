Key patterns

Three structural conditions that determined which companies AI destroyed — and which survived.

🧱
The moat was human labour
Every company on this list was a human-labour intermediary. When AI automated the labour itself, the entire business model became redundant overnight. Switching costs vanished.
💸
AI replaced paid with free
ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude give users what Chegg, Duolingo, and Shutterstock charged $13–$60/month for — at zero marginal cost. No amount of pivoting survives a free alternative.
⚡
Speed was unprecedented
Chegg acknowledged AI damage just 5 months after ChatGPT launched. Teleperformance lost 29% in one day. Previous tech disruptions (internet, mobile) played out over 5–10 years. AI: 5 months.
🔄
Self-cannibalization as survival
Shutterstock licensed its archive to OpenAI — training the tool that replaces it — for $104M. This is the new playbook: monetize your own obsolescence while you still can.
📉
Per-seat SaaS model is dying
AI agents replace human seats. Salesforce, Atlassian, ServiceNow, and Workday all face the same existential question: if AI does the work, why pay per person who uses the software?
🛡️
Regulated data = surviving moat
The companies most resistant to AI disruption hold regulated, proprietary data (tax records, healthcare). AI can't replace compliance requirements — only commoditize the UI layer on top.

The disruption is ongoing

Wix laid off 1,000 people yesterday. Chegg is fighting NYSE delisting. The BPO industry is restructuring in real-time. This list will keep growing.
